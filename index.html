OCD, Neil Hilborn

A starkly honest depiction of a relationship and its breakdown from the perspective of someone who suffers from Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder. Through rapidly delivered lines and stuttering repetition, Hilborn is able to convey the cyclic and obsessive thoughts which are characteristic of the disorder. Romantic attachment becomes manic infatuation and the anguish evoked by heartbreak. A written version of OCD can be found in his anthology, Our Numbered Days.

Manic Pixie Dream Girl, Olivia Gatwood

'Manic Pixie Dream Girl' was a term coined by the film critic Nathan Rabin in 2005 and describes any female character who “exists solely... to teach broodingly soulful young men to embrace life and its infinite mysteries and adventures.” In her poem, Gatwood calls attention to the one-sided nature of relationships between the male protagonist and this female archetype, revealing these types of characters to be a product of male fantasy and ill-aligned with realistic depictions of women. A written version of this poem can be found in Gatwood’s anthology, New American Best Friend.

14 Lines from Love Letters or Suicide Notes, Doc Luben

Luben's nervous delivery of the poem suits its quirky style, and the way in which in blends together comedy and tragedy. From the outset, the poem presents itself as dark humour, portraying ‘madly in love’ as indistinguishable from suicidal thoughts. Then, as Luben moves further down his list, his tone becomes more serious as the lines start to reflect on what it means to be human and the despair that usually comes with it. This change is tone is powerful and expected, eliciting an emotional reaction from the audience.

The Period Poem, Dominique Christina

Christina's delivery is unapologetic, fierce and at points humourous in its depiction of menstruation. The poem is dedicated to the poet’s young daughter, and seeks to override society’s fear and shame associated with it. A written version can be found in her anthology, They Are All Me.

A Letter to the Girl I Used to Be, Ethan Smith

Smith blends Spoken Word Poetry with the form of a letter to reflect on his experience of transitioning. By addressing his past self as 'Emily', he makes a distinction between the person he is today and the girl he was born as. While he stands by his decision to transition, stating that it "allowed her to become the man I am today", he expresses regret about no longer being able to bear children and also makes it clear that he feels no animosity towards 'Emily'. Therefore, this poem presents a nuanced and detailed depiction of being transgender.
